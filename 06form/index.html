History of Honduras

Pre-Columbian Era

The pre-Columbian civilizations were extraordinary developments in human society and culture, ranking with the early civilizations of Egypt, Mesopotamia, and China. Like the ancient urban societies of the Old World, those in the New World were characterized by kingdoms and empires, great monuments and cities, and refinements in the arts, metallurgy, and writing; the ancient civilizations of the Americas also display in their histories similar cyclical ...(100 of 53028 words). Before the arrival of Europeans, Honduras was inhabited by various indigenous groups, including the Maya, Lenca, and Pech. The Maya civilization, in particular, left a significant mark on the region with their impressive cities and advanced knowledge of astronomy and mathematics.

Spanish Colonization

In 1502, Christopher Columbus arrived in Honduras during his fourth voyage to the Americas. The Spanish quickly established control over the region, leading to the exploitation of indigenous peoples and resources. Honduras became part of the Spanish Empire and was governed as a province of the Captaincy General of Guatemala. When the Spanish arrived to colonize Honduras, the land was occupied by a variety of indigenous peoples, the most advanced of whom were the Maya. Gold stimulated Spanish conquest of the area early in the 16th century, and the Honduran gold-mining town of Gracias became the capital of Spanish Central America (the Audiencia de los Confines) in 1544. By 1548, however, the Spaniards had exhausted the gold, and Santiago (Antigua Guatemala) became the new capital of the Kingdom of Guatemala. Honduras, with its capital at Comayagua and agriculture the base of its economy, was a province of that kingdom (audiencia) within the Viceroyalty of New Spain.

Independence and Modern History

Independence from Spain came in 1821 and from Mexico in 1823, when Honduras joined in the formation of the United Provinces of Central America. Friction between Liberal and Conservative factions soon undermined the federation, however. In general, the Liberals favoured republicanism, freer trade, less government regulation, removal of the Catholic clergy’s political and economic powers, and imitation of foreign models of development. Conservatives defended the clergy, leaned toward monarchism, mistrusted foreign models, and were generally more traditional and pro-Spanish in their outlook. In 1830 a Honduran Liberal, Francisco Morazán, became president of this federation, and for a decade he promoted Liberal policies that curtailed the traditional power and privileges of the clergy and increased agricultural exports. Conservative and popular opposition to Liberal policies led to the collapse of the federation, and Honduras declared its absolute independence on November 5, 1838.

Facts about Honduras

  1. Honduras is located in Central America, bordered by Guatemala to the west, El Salvador to the southwest, Nicaragua to the southeast, and the Caribbean Sea to the north. Its diverse geography includes mountains, rainforests, and coastal areas.
  2. The capital city of Honduras is Tegucigalpa, often referred to as "Tegus." It is the largest city in the country.
  3. The official language of Honduras is Spanish, which is spoken by the majority of the population. Indigenous languages are also spoken by some communities, reflecting the country's rich cultural diversity.
  4. The currency of Honduras is the Honduran Lempira (HNL). The Lempira is named after a 16th-century indigenous leader who resisted Spanish conquest.
